Review text:
AI with Prof. Ansaf was solid. For context, I took it during the Fall 2023 semester. It served as a nice opportunity to learn concepts and strategies that serve as a basis for artificial intelligence and gradually transition to working with actual AI technology. The balance between coding and conceptual assignments also served as a way of putting these concepts we learned in lecture into practice (though they got a bit tedious at times). Though I’ve never attended her office hours, Prof. Ansaf seemed approachable. She also was fairly engaging as a lecturer.
Workload notes:
The class had 5 coding assignments and 5 conceptual assignments (50% total), and two exams throughout the semester (25%) that effectively served as “midterms” without any final when I took it. To my knowledge, this setup doesn’t apply anymore and the second exam had gotten pushed to later on in the semester and became a final. The assignments were staggered such that the conceptual assignment was always due before the accompanying coding assignment. The lowest coding and conceptual grades also got dropped at the time. The conceptual assignments involve calculations, short-answer, etc. related to the topics we went through for the course, while the coding assignments involved applying these concepts for Python code. The assignments we did were:
* comparing search algorithms (breadth-first search, depth-first search, etc.) for number sliding puzzles
* backtracking with optimization for solving sudoku puzzles 
* minimax for deciding a move for 2048
* using a neural network for sign language sign classification
* a 5th assignment that I unfortunately don’t have the file for at the moment as I skipped it (my apologies)

These assignments get reused between semesters, and I would say the 2048 algorithm was the most difficult, as it involved testing out different values as part of functions that you need to implement. The sudoku and number-sliding puzzles were the second-hardest, then the neural network one was the easiest out of the four I completed for the class. The exams, however, were pretty rough. I was able to do decently on the first with moderate studying for it, but I admittedly did much worse on the second since I couldn’t finish. However, the course gets curved accordingly, and these exams take up relatively low weight compared to other classes. To that end, the work is a lot more doable to be able to get an A compared to Prof. Dear, as well as other CS classes in the department – do what you will with that information…

Review text:
I took this course with Prof Dear but this review is more about the course than the professor.
COMS 4701 is very much "plug-and-chug" and leaves very little room for self-discovery. If this does not match your learning style, you will probably feel like you're wasting your time. Because there is so much hand-holding involved, it's easy to score well without doing all that much work. That said, I think this course is a great introductory course for non-majors or people just starting CS.
Material--very broad overview of AI and high-level discussions of concepts. To get a sense of what the book is like, you can just check out the Wikipedia page on "Artificial Intelligence" or any of the topics on the syllabus. You will not find much more depth in 4701.
Quizzes--the quizzes are mostly multiple choice and it's all open book. Extra study time generally is not rewarded as there's not that much depth involved. Also, expect some wrong multiple choice answers to be adversarial in nature so be prepared to second guess your response all the time.
Homework--About 75-80% of the code is written all ready, and the remaining code is not difficult to implement. Most of it was translating pseudocode into python. The short answer responses were also not terribly interesting. "Is this implementation more efficient than the other", "Is this guaranteed to find a solution", etc.
Grading--There is a curve, but it's probably not necessary since everyone is scoring in the 80s and 90s. The grading rubric is well defined and expectations for each assignment/quiz are clear. 
I would recommend this course to people without a background in CS or math. The professor is very knowledgeable and the TA's are helpful. Or, if you want an easy A, go for it.
