Over the past month, I’ve heard many of us question the purpose of this course. People asked: What are we doing here? Why are we learning SSH? Why do Makefiles have to be structured so precisely? Why do we have to use Git for homework?

The answer is that these are foundational skills for any competent developer. In real industry work, anything larger than our homework assignments, you need to connect to remote servers, collaborate through GitHub, and write code that follows consistent conventions and style. These are the hard requirements

I know it can feel tedious at first. When I first had to learn these tools on professional projects, I had to figure it out on my own. This course is meant to make that learning curve smoother.

At the same time, I'd argue Professor BB did not make C easy for us. I guess the course expects us to learn C in just a few weeks, which is quite demanding. Before the course I only knew Python and Java, have to say C is quite different.
Workload notes:
6 hours a week.

Review text:
I stepped into 3157 with the same fear as everyone else. I walked out with a stack of knowledge, countless tricks, and... still a lingering fear. It was an intense course, but with the right approach, it’s more than manageable.

Content:
Yes. Jae is *that* good. You've probably heard some horror stories—and I can confirm them—but his course design and lectures are truly exceptional. In the end, 
- You will learn a lot about C
- You will build a solid foundation in Git and UNIX
- You will be a very careful coder, thanks to his harsh grading :(
Are these useful? Not always, but think of it as a workout on your programming habit and attitude.
----------
Survival tips
- Attend lectures in-person, and take good notes
- Join Section 2 for extra help—it's where Jae shows his nice side (yes, it exists)
- Do the practice exams and review solutions carefully
- Start the labs EARLY. Debugging under pressure is not a fun sport.
- The TAs and ListServ are incredibly helpful!
Workload notes:
TLDR: "Grades may not be super accurate in terms of assessing your understanding." 
– Jae Lee, Day 1

Grading is HARSH and often binary. Just try your best.

----------
Assignments:
7 assignments (called labs), each with clear guidelines
- Lectures often hint at the general approach to a solution
- No crazy programming gymnastics needed—if you need something, he'll give you or tell you where to look. 
- Labs 6 and 7 are significantly longer but build on earlier labs
- Debugging is where the real time goes. The rubric is harsh, so precision matters.
- As always, start the labs EARLY. Not because they take forever, but reinforcing concepts early pays off in this fast-paced class.

----------
Exams:
Jae is an awesome exam writer, testing your understanding to the finest detail.
- The problems aren't impossible–everything comes directly from the lectures and labs. The real challenge lies in managing stress and focusing on meticulous details.
- Some problems offer generous partial credit. Others? Miss one part and the others crash down like an avalanche ...
- Practice helps, but luck plays a role too. At the end of the day, we are human, not computers, not gcc -g -Wall. We make mistakes, and that’s part of what makes us human.

----------
Grading

His grading system is statistically fair for a class with 400 students: on AVERAGE, good students do well, and bad students struggle. However, each of us only takes 3 exams, not 400, so this "fairness" may break down entirely on an individual scale.

The takeaway is: Always try your best, but never over-stress about your AP grade. It's your learning and improvement that matter. Your grade is just a number; it correlates with, but doesn’t always define, your abilities.

Also don’t lose sleep over an A+. It’s reserved for the top 3-ish unfortunately...

This class is INFAMOUS so you’ll go in scared. Jae will scare you a little more. Eventually, you’ll learn that it’s really not that bad but the tests are pretty deadly (do all 4 practice tests he provides and watch/attend all the TA review sessions. Go to class. 

This semester, like every other semester, there was a bit cheating scandal. A lot of people cheat and some of them get caught and threatened with disciplinary action.
Workload notes:
3 exams, and 7 labs. 6 and especially 7 are tough. Lots of people get busted for cheating on 6 and 7 and copying the code from the public repos on GitHub. Don’t do it because he’ll make you regret it. The labs are out of 87.5 (he’ll explain to you how he gets that number later). So the max you can get is 387.5/400, and the grade cutoffs are posted at the end. You won’t get an A+, you probably won’t get an A. After that, the curve gets very generous. The vast majority of people reading this will get in the B range. 

Difficulty is overhyped compared to other upper level classes, but I think it’s because this is meant to be a bridge to upper level classes. Borowski does baby you a bit and Jae does his best to make an upperclassman out of you. Even if you know a lot of the new stuff like networks and C memory management, you’ll still need to work hard and you will learn a lot (but especially if you don’t know the new stuff). Seriously, it’ll be like night and day. CS will start to make sense. Words that may have sounded like gibberish will become your new lingo. 

Finally, the cult of personality is real. You’ll love Jae sometimes, you’ll hate him other times, but most of all you’ll get why every Columbia student knows his name. In spite of everything, I still recommend him over Borowski unless you know all this stuff already or have a really rough semester scheduled. Unless you really care about having > 4.0 (rather than, let’s say, a 3.95) or can’t code in intro to Java/data structures without ChatGPT, there are no downsides to this class.
