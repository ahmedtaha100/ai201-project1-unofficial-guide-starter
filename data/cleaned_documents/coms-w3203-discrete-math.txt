Course: COMS W3203 Discrete Mathematics
Professor: Tony Dear
Rating: 4
Review text:
Professor Dear is a very approachable, very experienced lecturer who teaches discrete math in a structured way. It is very clear what you need to learn in order to succeed in the course, and I found his programming exercises very interesting and manageable.
Everything in this course (assignments, midterm, final) was extremely reasonable in terms of difficulty and workload. You might need to spend some extra time solving the assignments, but it is worth doing because the difficulty of exams closely mirrors the assignments. Final average was around 70%, but this is typical for a discrete math course because it's the first proof-based course for many. In my opinion, though, the exam was surprisingly light (some questions were trivial), and it felt almost too easy considering the fact he allowed us to bring 4 cheat sheets. If you put in the time understanding the assignment questions and carefully read through the Zybooks, you should be fine.
TLDR: course content is extremely easy, but exams can require some more careful thinking and problem-solving.
Workload notes:
6 Assignments, 1 Midterm, 1 Final. Prof uses Pensieve instead of GradeScope; the only difference between the two is that the former has built in Claude as an AI assistant for participation exercises.
I spent probably 1.5 hr on each HW, spent 7 hr total revising for each test.

Review text:
Tony Dear is a very clear and detail-oriented lecturer. He's cares about helping his students succeed, whether that be making extra office hours or a generous curve. However there is a large gap between the examples walked through during class and the homework problems, and a similarly large gap between homework problems and exam problems. Class examples are the foundations but for homework problems you need to learn how to apply your base knowledge. Also reviewing for exams is less about how many problems you do and more about deeply understanding the concepts and solutions for the problems you do. Discrete problems require a level of creativity, so it's more important to just understand what's going on than to crank out extra practice. Problem sets were pretty time-consuming and for harder units like Mod I had to self-study external material to understand. Definitely utilize your resrouces: go to TA and professor office hours. He's hard but fair. I would say take him if you are generally curious, like a challenge, and don't mind putting in the time outside of class to deeply understand the content. If you want an easy A, take Discrete with Ansaf. Final exam was extremely hard, ~110/150 is a good score for an A typically.
Workload notes:
Problem sets took ~6 hours/week. Exams took ~8-10 hours of review.

Review text:
Everyone says that the class is really hard—and to be honest, it is quite a bit of work. However, I wouldn’t go as far as to say you should avoid Professor Dear at all costs. Discrete Math is an introduction to proof-based mathematics, so it's no surprise that it feels challenging at first. That said, Professor Dear's class is genuinely informative, which is especially valuable if you plan to do computer science in the future. The course follows a textbook, though a few chapters are skipped. In a nutshell, it was a tough but interesting class with a professor who is eager to explain topics you don’t understand
Workload notes:
There were a total of seven problem sets, assigned roughly every two weeks. The exams were difficult and often went beyond the level of the homework and recitation questions, this gap became more noticeable as the semester progressed. The median score dropped significantly from the first midterm to the final. However, since the class is graded on a curve, it didn’t impact final grades as harshly as it might have otherwise.
The main advice for anyone planning to take this class with Professor Tony Dear is simple: show up. Attend every lecture and recitation, start working on the homework early, and make use of office hours. It’s extremely difficult to succeed in this class without putting in the work—unless you already have a strong background in proof-based mathematics. On average, I spent about 12 hours per week on this class. Sometimes it was less, but during exam weeks, it could be as much as 20 hours.
