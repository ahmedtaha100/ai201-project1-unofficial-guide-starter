For Intro to Java, you have to purchase a subscription to ZyBooks, which was annoying. I was glad that we did not have to for Data Structures. Review text:
If you've taken COMS1004 before (or know Java) the transition into this class will be much easier than if you don't, so absolutely make sure you have some understanding of Java. That being said, Blaer's class is good as it doesn't make Data Structures any harder than it needs to be; it's certainly not a weeder class. Much like his Intro to Java class, attendance is optional and everything you need is posted online or is explained in depth the textbook, you can effectively treat this like an online class with three in person exams. However be warned that this fact does not make Data Structures as a subject any easier, and most people stumble by not taking the class as seriously as they need to. Attend/watch lectures live and ask pointed questions (or engage with Blaers questions). As while Blaer is a decent lecturer you'll find more value in what he has to say if you probe him for a bit more information. Make honest initial attempts on the homeworks despite their length, and clarify things with the TA's (the ones that are nice). The textbook is accessible online, and while you wont need it half the time if you watch lecture, I recommend using it to bolster your understanding as Blaer begins to blitz through certain topics after the first midterm and complete. This is a very manageable class which will reward you if you put the time in, and compared to the other Data Structure's sections I would say Blaer's is your best choice for the least painful experience. Don't go delaying Data Structures by a semester for Blaer though he's not THAT good.
Workload notes:
5 Homework Pairs of Coding and Written sections released separately but treated together, they can be very large/confusing but you'll also be given ample time to complete them: worth 40%. Two Mid-Terms worth 15% each, the first one is easier than the second content wise. The final constitutes the remaining 30%. The course load averages out to about 7 Hours a week, however some weeks you'll find yourself just watching lectures (if even), and others you'll need to spend an entire day doing a homework. Class is curved up to a B+ and the typical uncurved average is a B-.

Review text:
Everyone who says the grading is transparent and clear has never actually programmed for a real-life project. Programming is an art, in every sense of the word. Artists have their freedom to create to their heart's desire, so long as their client approves of the piece. Such a statement implies that an artist is in direct communication with the client and can receive instructions and clarifications in what is essentially real-time. Even in programming interviews, you can submit your work, get feedback, and resubmit with no penalty. Your IDE handles syntax errors and spacing, allowing a programmer to focus on how the math and logic creates results.
Dr. B does his best to stamp the artistic freedom out of this process. He poses questions on how well you can remember syntax. He tests you on how well you can remember his exact implementation - any variation, even if it leads to the same, correct answer - is not allowed. As an aside, his coding style is to aim for code that looks clean at the expense if the readability of the algorithm. His slides give information that are directly contrary to what he expects from us.
I will concede that he is a decent lecturer. All of that, however, is offset by just how badly his tests and assignments are implemented. And what use is all the theory if his practicals don't allow you to try anything?
Workload notes:
Assignments vary in length. One assignment took a full 8 hours after even the TAs conceded that the expected solution was not taught in class and was not present in any of the course material. Tests contain trick questions that never appear in real life. The 'Capstone Project' is no different from a regular assignment, and even though it is called a "project," there is still only one implementation!

8 programming assignments (7% of grade, lowest dropped), 4 theoretical assignments (8%), 6 quizzes (5%, lowest dropped), 2 midterms (34% each), and capstone programming assignment (12%).

Everything was judged so semantically that if your answer wasn’t exactly what Dr. B had in mind, it was marked wrong. Regrade requests? Good luck, you’ll have better odds convincing a compiler to “just chill.”
Exams were another adventure: questions often came from topics mentioned for about 12 seconds at the end of lecture. Thankfully, cheat sheets were allowed, which turned the test into a scavenger hunt through your own notes.
